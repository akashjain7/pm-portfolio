The brief

What is Orthians Polycraft?

Orthians Polycraft was a D2C memory foam ergonomic accessories brand I co-founded with a partner. The idea was simple: Indians spending long hours sitting at desks, in cars, on couches, were developing chronic back, neck, and posture problems. The market had players, but no one was offering genuinely premium product quality at an accessible price point.

We planned to enter with a focused memory foam range, including neck pillows, car back supports, lumbar cushions, and seat cushions and expand into the full memory foam home accessories niche over time.

Core thesis: Better product, premium memory foam density + superior fabric quality at the same price as the market leader (Frido). Possible because we have in-house manufacturing, which Frido doesn't. The cost savings went into product quality, not margins.

Market context

Why this market?

The Indian ergonomic accessories market is at an early but fast-moving inflection point. Three tailwinds converged to make a compelling entry window.

Tailwind 1 — Remote work + long sitting hours
Post-COVID, a large cohort of urban 20–35 year olds permanently shifted to hybrid or WFH setups. Hours spent sitting at desks jumped sharply. Back pain and neck pain went from a middle-aged problem to a mainstream 25-year-old problem.
Tailwind 2 — D2C memory foam is proven in India
Frido validated the market. They've raised $28.9M and have a strong Amazon presence. The category has consumer trust. The question wasn't "will people buy this online?", it was "is there a better product they'd switch to?"
Tailwind 3 — Health awareness among Tier 1 youth
The 20–35 urban Indian cohort is the most health-aware generation in Indian history, tracking steps, buying ergonomic furniture, and watching posture content. Memory foam accessories fit naturally into this lifestyle shift.
Market size signal
Global memory foam pillow market was valued at $2.87B in 2024, growing at 8% CAGR. India's ergonomic products space is a fast-growing subset of this, led by online-first D2C brands. Frido's $28.9M funding is the clearest local validation signal.

User research

Who we were building for

Our research combined Amazon and Flipkart review mining, Reddit and Twitter conversations around back pain and WFH ergonomics, and direct conversations with potential buyers. Two primary personas emerged.

R

Works 9–10 hours/day at a desk. Started noticing neck stiffness 6 months ago.
Problem
Chronic neck and lower back pain from long desk hours. Has tried cheap pillows that didn't last. Willing to spend ₹2,000–3,000 for something that actually works.
Decision trigger
A bad week of pain, or a colleague recommending a product. Research on Amazon, checks reviews obsessively before buying. Cares about whether it'll actually last.
What he wants
Something that feels premium, actually helps posture, and looks decent on his desk. Not a medical-looking product, wants it to feel like a lifestyle upgrade.
Why Orthians fits
Premium fabric (visible quality signal), better foam density (functional difference), same price as what he's already considering spending on Frido.
P
Priya, 31: Product manager, Delhi
Drives 45 mins daily to office. Lower back pain has been worsening.
Problem
Long commute + desk job = full-day back stress. Has bought a car seat cushion once, it flattened in 3 months. Frustrated with products that don't last.
Decision trigger
Searches "best car lumbar support India" or sees a targeted Instagram ad. Buys fast when the product looks premium and reviews are strong.
What she wants
Durability above all. Something that maintains shape after months of use. Premium feel matters; she'd gift this to her husband too, if it looks good.
Why Orthians fits

VOC synthesis

What customers said about existing products

Review mining across Amazon and Flipkart for Frido, Sleepsia, and Dr.Trust revealed consistent patterns in what buyers praised and what they complained about.

What buyers praised
  • Immediate comfort on first use
  • Relief from neck/back pain within days
  • Easy to wash covers
  • Value for money at ₹1,500–2,000 range
What buyers complained about
  • Foam flattening within 3–6 months
  • Cover fabric feels cheap / rough
  • Car supports don't fit all seat types
  • Poor after-sales and return experience
The insight that shaped our product: Buyers weren't unhappy with the category; they were unhappy with how quickly products degraded. "Great for 2 months, then useless" was the recurring pattern. Better foam density (higher durability) and premium fabric (better feel over time) directly addressed the top two complaints. This wasn't guesswork; it was the product brief that came out of the VOC work.
Product strategy

Positioning and product decisions

Every positioning decision we made flowed from a single strategic anchor: we had a structural cost advantage through in-house manufacturing. Most competitors outsourced production, which meant their cost-to-quality ratio was fixed. Ours wasn't. We could put the savings directly into better raw materials, higher-density memory foam and premium outer fabric without raising prices.

The positioning in one line: "The premium memory foam product you'd expect to pay more for at exactly what you'd pay for Frido."
Product decisions

What to launch first and why

We decided to launch with a focused set of three SKUs rather than the full range, to keep inventory risk low and allow us to build reviews and trust before expanding.

1
Neck pillow — Launch hero product
Highest search volume in the category. Clearest pain point. Easiest to demonstrate quality difference (foam density + fabric feel are immediately noticeable). Lower manufacturing complexity vs seat cushions. Entry price point of ₹1,800–2,200 is accessible but not cheap.
2
Car lumbar support — Second SKU
Strong commuter use case. Underserved in terms of quality, most car seats in this range are too thin or lose shape quickly. High gifting potential (car accessories make sense as gifts). Slightly higher price point: ₹2,500–3,500.
3
Seat / coccyx cushion — Third SKU
Strong WFH and office desk use case. Repeat purchase potential: people buy one for home, one for office. Works as a bundle with the neck pillow. ₹1,500–2,000 price point drives volume.
Brand decisions

How we wanted Orthians to feel

Visual identity direction
Clean, warm, premium. Not clinical or medical-looking (that's Dr. Trust's lane). Not playful or youth-targeted (that's a budget brand signal). Think: the ergonomic equivalent of a premium stationery brand. Natural tones, confident typography, product photography that shows texture.
Tone of voice
Knowledgeable but not preachy. Honest about what the product does. No vague claims like "scientifically designed" without specifics. Instead: "720g/m³ foam density doesn't flatten after 3 months." Show the specs, let the product speak.
Key brand rule: Every claim on the website had to be product-grounded. Density numbers, fabric certifications, and durability test results. This was the trust signal that would differentiate us from brands making generic "orthopedic expert designed" claims.

The website was built to do three things: tell the product story, build trust, and convert. Every structural decision was made from VOC, based on what buyers said they needed to feel confident before spending ₹2,000+ on a pillow they couldn't touch in a store.

Success criteria

How we'd have measured success

These were the metrics I defined before launch, what a successful first 6 months would look like, and the signals that would tell us our positioning thesis was working.

Metric Target (Month 3) Target (Month 6) Why it matters
Amazon rating ≥ 4.2 stars ≥ 4.4 stars Primary trust signal for new buyers. Below 4.0 kills conversion.
Review volume 50+ reviews 200+ reviews Below 50, buyers don't trust the product. 200+ enables organic ranking.
Return rate < 8% < 6% High returns signal product-market fit failure or size/fit mismatch.
D2C site conversion 1.5% 2.5% Industry benchmark is 1–3%. Below 1% = trust or UX problem.
Repeat purchase rate — 15%+ Validates durability claim, if the product lasts, users don't need to rebuy quickly.
Blended CAC ≤ ₹400 ≤ ₹300 With ASP of ₹2,000, CAC above ₹500 kills unit economics.
Organic traffic share 10% 25% SEO investment should start reducing paid dependency by Month 4–5.
North star metric: Amazon rating × review volume. Both had to move together. High rating with low reviews is fragile, high reviews with declining rating means product-market fit is slipping. The combination was our real pulse check in the first 6 months.

Honest reflection

What I learned and what I'd do differently

Orthians didn't launch. That's the reality. But the work was real, the thinking was real, and the reasons it didn't happen taught me more about what actually matters in a 0→1 build than any successful launch would have.

Why it didn't launch
We had the strategy, the product vision, and the manufacturing backing. What we didn't have was the working capital to fund the first inventory run while simultaneously building the marketing flywheel. D2C requires cash for inventory, for ads, for the time it takes reviews and SEO to compound. We underestimated how long the runway needed to be before the business could be self-sustaining.
What I'd do differently: validate before building
I'd spend the first 30 days selling manually over WhatsApp, Instagram DMs, and local offices before building a website or listing on Amazon. Even 20 paid sales with real feedback would have sharpened the product brief dramatically and de-risked the inventory decision. We jumped to infrastructure (website, GTM plan) before validating that people would actually pay for this product.
What I'd do differently: launch with one SKU, not three
The plan to launch three SKUs simultaneously was a capital and focus mistake. One product, done exceptionally well, would have been cheaper to inventory, easier to market, and faster to build reviews on. Spread across three SKUs, the review count per product would have been too thin to generate Amazon ranking momentum in the first 90 days.
What worked: the positioning thesis was real
The core bet: Better product at the same price, enabled by in-house manufacturing, was genuinely differentiated. Frido's review sections are full of "good but flattens too fast" complaints. That's a real gap. The product strategy wasn't the problem. The business infrastructure around it was.
What this taught me about PM thinking
Strategy without a clear path to validation is just a document. The most important PM skill I took from this isn't market sizing or GTM planning, it's knowing the fastest, cheapest way to find out if you're wrong about your core assumption. We should have answered "will someone pay ₹2,000 for our product?" before we answered "how do we scale to 1,000 orders a month."
If I were to relaunch

What the v2 plan would look like

Different: Start with services revenue
Corporate wellness tie-ups, sell in bulk to companies setting up WFH kits for employees. B2B sales generate cash flow without marketplace dependency and serve as proof of product quality for B2C launch.
Different: One SKU, one channel first
Neck pillow only. Amazon only. Get to 100 reviews with ≥4.3 stars before spending a rupee on ads or building a website. Let the marketplace do the discovery work before investing in owned channels.
Same: The product quality bet
Better foam density, better fabric, same price as Frido. This positioning is still right. The customer insight "products flatten too fast" hasn't changed. The manufacturing advantage is still real.
Same: Full-online GTM
No offline retail for at least 18 months. The discovery and trust-building infrastructure of Amazon + Meta is the right environment for a new D2C brand in this category.
The PM takeaway: The gap between a good strategy and a launched product is execution infrastructure; capital, sequencing, and validation. A PM's job is to compress the distance between "we think this will work" and "we know this works." I learned that lesson the hard way here, and it's shaped how I think about product prioritisation and de-risking ever since.
